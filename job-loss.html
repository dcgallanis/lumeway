What to expect

A timeline of what
needs to happen

Day 1

Immediate steps

  • Review your termination or separation paperwork carefully
  • Do not sign anything immediately — you usually have time to review
  • Ask for a copy of your employee file and any performance reviews
  • Note your last day of health insurance coverage
First week

Protect your benefits

  • File for unemployment insurance in your state (don't wait)
  • Elect COBRA health insurance or find alternative coverage
  • Review severance agreement with an employment attorney if offered
  • Request your final paycheck and any unused PTO payout
First month

Financial protection

  • Build a lean budget based on unemployment income
  • Contact creditors proactively if you anticipate payment issues
  • Decide what to do with your 401k (leave, roll over, or cash out)
  • Use FSA funds before coverage ends
Ongoing

Next chapter

  • Update your resume, LinkedIn, and professional references
  • Expand your network — most jobs come through connections
  • Consider skills training or certification if pivoting careers
  • Track job search expenses — some may be tax deductible

Critical deadlines

Don't miss these
time-sensitive steps

ASAP
File for unemployment

File within the first 1-2 weeks of job loss. Benefits don't back-pay to your last day of work — every week you wait is money lost.

60 days
COBRA health insurance

You have 60 days from loss of coverage to elect COBRA. Missing this window means losing access to continuation coverage entirely.

60 days
401k rollover

If you take a 401k distribution, you have 60 days to roll it into an IRA or new employer plan to avoid taxes and penalties.

Before signing
Severance review

If offered severance, you typically have 21 days to review the agreement. Workers over 40 have 21 days by law under the ADEA.

End of coverage
FSA funds

Flexible spending account funds may be forfeited when your coverage ends. Use them for eligible expenses before your last day.

Varies
Non-compete agreements

Review any non-compete clauses in your employment agreement before accepting a new job. Enforcement varies significantly by state.
