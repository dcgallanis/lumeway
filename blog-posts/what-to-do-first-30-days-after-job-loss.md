Losing a job can feel like the ground disappearing beneath you. Whether it was a layoff, a restructuring, or something you didn't see coming, the first 30 days are critical. This guide walks you through exactly what to do — one step at a time.

## Week 1: Stabilize

### File for Unemployment Immediately

Don't wait. File your unemployment claim with your state's Department of Labor as soon as possible. Processing times vary by state, and benefits are not retroactive in most cases.

You'll need:
- Your Social Security number
- Your employer's name and address
- Your last pay stub or W-2
- Your bank account information for direct deposit

### Review Your Severance Package

If your employer offered severance, read every line before signing. Pay attention to:
- How many weeks of pay you're receiving
- Whether your health insurance continues (and for how long)
- Non-compete or non-solicitation clauses
- Whether you're waiving your right to sue

Consider consulting an employment attorney if anything feels off — many offer free initial consultations.

### Understand Your Health Insurance Options

You typically have 60 days to elect COBRA continuation coverage, but don't wait until the deadline. Your options include:
- **COBRA**: Continues your employer plan, but you pay the full premium (often $500–$700/month for individuals)
- **ACA Marketplace**: Job loss is a qualifying life event — you get a 60-day Special Enrollment Period
- **Medicaid**: If your income drops low enough, you may qualify immediately
- **Spouse's plan**: If your partner has employer coverage, you can join during their next enrollment or via special enrollment

## Week 2: Get Organized

### Audit Your Finances

Before you start applying for jobs, get a clear picture of where you stand:
- List all monthly expenses (rent, utilities, subscriptions, debt payments)
- Calculate how many months your savings will cover
- Identify expenses you can cut or pause immediately
- Contact lenders about hardship programs if needed

### Gather Your Documents

Start collecting everything you'll need for job applications, benefits, and tax purposes:
- Last 3 pay stubs
- W-2 from the current year (or prior year)
- Performance reviews and recommendation letters
- Any certifications or training records
- A copy of your signed separation agreement

### Update Your Resume and LinkedIn

While everything is fresh, update your resume with your most recent role, accomplishments, and metrics. Update your LinkedIn headline to signal you're open to opportunities (there's a setting for this that only recruiters can see).

## Week 3: Build Your Plan

### Set a Daily Routine

Job searching without structure leads to burnout. Set a schedule:
- Morning: Apply to 2–3 jobs
- Midday: Network (reach out to one person per day)
- Afternoon: Skill-building or interview prep

### Tap Your Network

Most jobs are filled through connections, not job boards. Let people know you're looking:
- Email former colleagues and mentors
- Attend industry meetups or virtual events
- Join relevant LinkedIn groups and contribute to discussions

### Explore Retraining Options

If you're considering a career shift, look into:
- Your state's workforce development programs (often free)
- Community college certificates
- Online platforms with employer-recognized credentials

## Week 4: Protect Your Future

### Check Your Credit Report

Job loss can lead to missed payments if you're not careful. Pull your free credit report at AnnualCreditReport.com and set up alerts for any changes.

### Review Your Benefits Timeline

Mark these dates on your calendar:
- COBRA election deadline (60 days from coverage loss)
- ACA Special Enrollment Period (60 days from job loss)
- Last day of employer-paid benefits
- Date your severance pay ends
- Unemployment recertification schedule

### Consider Professional Help

If you're feeling overwhelmed, that's normal. Many communities offer:
- Free career counseling through workforce centers
- Sliding-scale therapy through community health centers
- Support groups for people in career transition

## You're Not Starting Over — You're Starting Informed

The first 30 days after job loss aren't about finding a new job immediately. They're about stabilizing your finances, protecting your benefits, and building a plan you can execute with confidence.
