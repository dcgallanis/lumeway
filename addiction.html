The supporter's journey

Five phases of
finding your way

Each phase has specific steps. Lumeway helps you know what to do — and what to avoid — at every stage.

1
Something's Wrong

First signs

  • You've noticed changes — missed obligations, personality shifts, financial red flags
  • Learn what this condition actually is before confronting anything
  • Start documenting what you observe — dates, behaviors, patterns
  • Call the SAMHSA helpline (1-800-662-4357) just to talk — no commitment
2
The Crisis Point

When it escalates

  • If they are hospitalized, ask the care team about treatment options before discharge
  • Plan the conversation — or decide if professional support makes sense
  • Set one clear boundary and communicate it
  • Secure valuables, medications, and important documents in your home
3
Getting Them Help

Navigating treatment

  • Verify health insurance coverage for treatment
  • Research treatment options: inpatient, outpatient, PHP, IOP
  • Call treatment centers — ask about programs, specializations, availability
  • Prepare essential documents in case they agree to go suddenly
4
While They're in Treatment

Your recovery starts too

  • Participate in the family program if the treatment center offers one
  • Continue attending Al-Anon or your own support group
  • Prepare the home environment before they return
  • Take care of yourself — sleep, eat, do things that bring you peace
5
The Long Road

Ongoing recovery

  • Support their aftercare plan without policing it
  • Learn the warning signs of setbacks
  • Build a response plan together — what happens if they slip, who to call
  • Continue your own recovery work — this changed your life too

Time-sensitive steps

What needs to happen and when

Immediately
Hospital discharge planning

If they are hospitalized, ask the care team about treatment options BEFORE they leave. Once they are discharged, the window of willingness often closes.

24-72 hours
SAMHSA helpline call

Call 1-800-662-4357. Free, confidential, available 24/7. They can help you understand your options and find local resources. You do not need to have a plan first.

1-2 weeks
Insurance verification

Call the number on the back of the insurance card and ask about coverage for treatment. Understanding benefits before choosing a program prevents surprise bills.

Ongoing
Support group attendance

Al-Anon and Nar-Anon meetings are for YOU — the supporter. Weekly attendance is recommended. You do not need to share. Just listen.

30-90 days
Treatment center research

Inpatient programs fill up. Having a researched list of 3-5 programs ready means you can act quickly when there is an opening or a moment of willingness.

Before return
Home environment preparation

Remove substances from the home, establish routines, and discuss expectations before they come back from treatment. Preparation prevents early setbacks.

Look for behavioral changes (secrecy, defensiveness, missed obligations), physical signs (appearance changes, sleep disruption), and financial red flags (unexplained withdrawals, unpaid bills). Traditional confrontational interventions can backfire. Consider the CRAFT method (Community Reinforcement and Family Training) — an evidence-based approach that helps families encourage treatment without confrontation. If you do pursue a formal intervention, work with a professional interventionist.
You cannot force someone into recovery. Focus on what you can control: set boundaries, attend Al-Anon or Nar-Anon meetings, and take care of your own health. Your wellbeing matters regardless of their choices.
Treatment options include inpatient (residential), outpatient, PHP (partial hospitalization), and IOP (intensive outpatient). Look for CARF or Joint Commission accreditation, ask about dual diagnosis capabilities, and verify insurance coverage before choosing. Use FindTreatment.gov to search programs near you.
